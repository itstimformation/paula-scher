Paula Scher is a multi-award winning designer whose career has spanned over four decades. She is well-known for her environmental graphics, work as an educator, and her work at the world’s largest design consultancy, Pentagram. When she was just five years old she remembers seeing an abstract cover for South Pacific and this made her curious about design. However, with no artists in her family, Scher didn’t truly ‘find’ herself until she discovered a graphic design course. From making paper dolls and comic strips in school, to serving on the Art Commission for the city of New York, Scher has made a huge impact through her work and life.

Early Life

On October 6 1948, Paula Scher was born in Washington DC, Virginia. Growing up as a child she moved house numerous times, from DC to Arlington, then Fairfax County, before finally settling in Silver Spring, Maryland where she attended Silver Spring Elementary School. Scher found it hard growing up in a family and had an unhappy childhood, one where no one had the same passion for art as she did. She would draw a lot and used this as a way to go off to her room and escape everyone else. As a result this hindered the relationship she had with her parents for her child years. While at home, Schers father was a photogrammetric engineer who worked for the US Geological Survey. The USGS study the landscape of the USA and also look at any natural hazards that could have a negative effect on the country. During his time working there, he developed a device known as Stereo Templates in the basement of the family home. This basically corrected any lens distortion that took place in aerial photography. This allowed the government to create more accurate maps of the country and has played a major part in the development of Google Maps. After this huge development, he gave his invention to the Government for a mere $1500.

Education

Scher attended high school in Silver Springs and while there she also attended additional art classes at Corcoran College of Art and Design. Due to her obvious talent and passion, she ended up being the high schools publicity chairman and created all the posters and advertising for all the school events, she was nicknamed ‘the school artist’. In a recent interview Scher laughed as she described how she created the posters for all the events she never attended.

In 1966, during the time of the Vietnam War, Scher attended Tyler School of Art in Pennsylvania. She originally wanted to draw however quickly found out that she couldn’t draw overly well. Scher tried many different things before discovering graphic design in her junior year.

“Helvetica is the font of the Vietnam War”

The first year of graphic design was taught in the Basel method which required you to be net in your work, however Scher admitted to having a lack of neatness skills and this idea made her feel like she was being forced to “tidy her room”. She found that her ideas were much stronger than her ability to draw. All of this made the decision for her to switch major in design. In doing so she had classes with Stephen Tarantal. His classes focused on ideas. But, when the course split, she chose illustration. It was during this time that Scher met one of her mentors who would influence the rest of her life as a designer. Stanislaw Zagorski’s classes combined both illustration and design where Scher had always solved problems illustratively. Stanislaw told her during his classes, “illustrate with type”. To this day, Scher says that this was the best advice she had received and immediately began to illustrate through the use of type. A lot of her education was rooted in the Swiss international style of typography.

“Words have meaning, type has spirit”

In 1970, Paula Scher graduated Tyler School of Art with a Bachelor of Fine Arts and mentors that would inspire her for a lifetime.

Career

After graduating with a BFA, Scher moved to New York City carrying with her no more than $50 and her portfolio. She spent two years living in different suburbs with numerous friends. This experience inspired her to write her later children’s book ‘The Brownstone’. Her first job she got was designing the insides of children’s books for Random House after meeting the Art Director, Harris Lewine. Through this connection, Scher met her future husband, Seymour Chwast who at the time was an established partner at Push Pin Studios. They married in 1973 but were divorced five years later. However, after having some “time off for bad behaviour”, they remarried in 1989.

“I wasn't a very good illustrator so I became a designer.”

In 1972 Scher got a job within the promotions department at CBS records all the time wanting to work in the record over department as those artists were considered ‘better’. After working in promotions for two years, she made a move to Atlantic records as an art director. After just a year gaining experience, Scher moved back to CBS as an art director and ended up working there for 8 years and was credited for designing upwards of 150 covers a year. Aside from her covers, she was credited with reviving numerous historical typefaces and design styles. When creating album covers, Scher found she preferred to use a mood or mysterious scenario over a literal description of artists. The economic crash during the 1970’s had a large impact on her mature work. The crash pushed her to rely much more heavily on letterforms over illustrations and portray content and identity via typography. In later years, this style became her hallmark.

Scher approached her work from what she called a populist viewpoint. This encompasses both popular culture with the goal of engaging people with the cover art in order to interest them enough to buy the record.

“That early foundation was very important in solidifying how I think about things, even though styles and technologies have changed”

In 1982, Scher left the record industry in order to pursue a new career of her own company, partnered with her old school friend, Terry Koppel who at the time was a popular magazine designer. The studios of Koppel and Scher was a balance of editorial design along with promotion packaging and covers. The company lasted for about 7 years until the first Gulf War hit in 1990 along with a recession. This economic downturn meant for Scher and Koppel, there were no magazines to design. Terry Koppel moved to esquire and left Scher to run the business.

In 1991 Woody Pirtle, a partner at Pentagram Design Consultancy, came to Scher’s studio and approached her about joining Pentagram. From there, Scher became a Principal at the New York City offices.

In 1992, Scher also became an educator when she began teaching at the School of Visual Arts in New York City. As well as this Scher taught in Cooper Union. Yale University, and returned to Tyler School of Arts.

“My mother made me get my teaching certificate so there would be something to fall back on.”

Scher has won more than 300 awards from many international design associations. She is a select member of AGI – Alliance Graphique Internationale and her work is featured in collections within MoMA, the Library of Congress, Museum Für Gestaltung, and the Centre George Pompidou.

Reputable Works

In 1994, Scher became the first designer to create a new identity and promotional graphics for the theatre. The theatres numbers were decreasing and the challenge for Scher was to raise public awareness and attendance. A well-known and widely recognised poster was of Savion Glovers ‘Bring on Da Noise, Bring on Da Funk’. From 1993 to 2005, she worked closely with the producer at The Public Theatre, George C Wolfe on the further development of posters, ads, and the theatres very distinct identities.

In 1994, Scher created the first poster campaign for the festival which takes place annually in Central Park. The overall identity and visual language used during the campaign laid the foundation that defined The Public Theatre. Schers posters became a tradition in the city each year.

The Citi logo has been widely seen as one of the most recognisable and iconic bank logos in the world. The initial sketch was made by her during a client meeting. It was hastily sketched on a restaurant napkin. The red arc over the ‘t’ incorporates the Travellers logo, Travellers being the company that merged with Citibank.

“It took me a few seconds to draw it, but it took me 34 years to learn how to draw it in a few seconds”

Awards

Over a career of more than 4 decades, Paula Scher has won many different awards. Here are a selection of the awards she has won.

Type Directors Club Medal

This is awarded to individuals that have made significant advances for, contributions to, and achievements in the art and craft of type and typography.

Chrysler Design Award

Achievements of individuals in innovative works of architecture and design which influenced modern American culture.

Other awards include:-

Art Directors Club Hall of Fame

Medallist, American Institute of Graphic Arts

Doctor of Fine Arts Honoris Causa, Corcoran College of Art and Design

2001 AIGA Medal: Distinction in identity design, packaging design, publication design, environmental graphics

“The goal of design is to raise the expectation of what design can be”

Paula Scher has contributed greatly to the advancement of graphic design through her revival of typefaces and historical designs, as well as creating new styles based on Art Deco and Russian Constructivism. She has inspired many artists and her work will continue to do so for many years to come. Her work and passion has undoubtedly raised the expectation and standards for all designers and artists across the world.
